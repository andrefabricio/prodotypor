# 1. About the Author & Origin of the Book

## 1.1 Ken Watanabe — Background

Ken Watanabe is a Japanese author, educator, and entrepreneur. Before writing Problem Solving 101, he spent six years as a consultant at McKinsey & Company, one of the world's leading management consulting firms, where he worked with major corporations globally to solve complex business challenges using structured analytical tools.

In 2007, when Japan's prime minister placed education at the top of the national agenda, Watanabe left McKinsey to dedicate himself to education reform. His mission: shift Japan from 'memorization-focused education' to 'problem-solving-focused education' by packaging McKinsey's elite problem-solving tools in an approachable, practical format accessible to children.

After the book's success, Watanabe founded Delta Studios, his own education, media, and entertainment company in Tokyo, where he continues to work with children using real-world business simulations (including a 1965 VW van food business) to teach problem-solving experientially.

## 1.2 The Book's Origins & Impact

Problem Solving 101 was originally written in Japanese for schoolchildren in 2007 and published by Diamond Inc. To Watanabe's surprise, it became Japan's #1 Business Bestseller, with more than 370,000 copies sold in the first six months — driven not by children, but by adult executives, parents, teachers, and CEOs who had never been taught structured problem-solving.

The English edition (Portfolio/Penguin, 2009) brought the same tools to global audiences. Seth Godin called it a book that would make the world a better place if everyone read it. Dan Ariely praised its ability to help readers recognize common elements in everyday decisions.

# 2. The Problem-Solving Mindset

## 2.1 The Five Archetypes — Who Solves Problems and Who Doesn't

Watanabe introduces five character archetypes to illustrate the range of human responses to challenges. The first four represent common anti-patterns; the fifth is the ideal. Understanding these archetypes helps identify behavioral blocks and reframe one's own approach.

| **Archetype**           | **Core Behavior**                                                                                           | **Key Failure Pattern**                                    | **How They Talk**                            |
|---|---|---|---|
| Miss Sigh               | Gives up immediately; blames others; fears failure and judgment                                             | Never starts; accepts defeat before trying                 | "I'll never be able to do that."             |
| Mr. Critic              | Points out every flaw in others' plans; never contributes solutions or effort                               | Talks instead of does; avoids personal accountability      | "I told you so."                             |
| Miss Dreamer            | Generates ideas enthusiastically but never builds actionable plans                                          | Lives in the idea stage; never reaches execution           | "Wouldn't it be great if..."                 |
| Mr. Go-Getter           | Takes immediate action with energy but skips analysis; attributes failure only to insufficient effort       | Acts without diagnosing; keeps running in wrong directions | "I've got to try harder!"                    |
| **Problem-Solving Kid** | **Sets clear goals; diagnoses root causes; builds action plans; executes and adjusts; learns from results** | **None — this is the model**                               | **"What really caused this? Let's fix it."** |
|---|---|---|---|

## 2.2 What Distinguishes a Problem-Solving Kid

Problem-solving kids are defined not by exceptional intelligence or talent, but by a specific set of behaviors and mental habits:

- They set clear, specific, time-bound goals rather than vague aspirations.

- They diagnose root causes before jumping to solutions.

- They maintain a positive, proactive attitude focused on what can be changed.

- They build concrete action plans and execute immediately.

- They monitor progress continuously and adjust based on feedback.

- They learn from both successes and failures, treating each outcome as data.

## 2.3 The Compound Growth Principle

Watanabe illustrates why continuous improvement matters through a watermelon-stand analogy. Three vendors each start selling 100 watermelons per month. After 3 years:

| **Monthly Growth Rate** | **Watermelons/Month After 3 Years** | **Multiplier** |
|---|---|---|
| 1% (Alex)               | 143                                 | 1.43x          |
| 5% (Bianca)             | 579                                 | 5.79x          |
| 10% (Cliff)             | 3,091                               | 30.91x         |

The takeaway: small, consistent improvements in problem-solving ability compound dramatically over time. A 10% monthly improvement rate produces 22x more output than a 1% rate after just 3 years. This is why developing problem-solving habits — not just solving individual problems — is so powerful.

# 3. The Four-Step Problem-Solving Process

The core methodology of Problem Solving 101 is a four-step process that applies universally — from improving math grades to restructuring a company. Watanabe emphasizes that these steps form a package: all four must be completed; thinking and acting must be combined.

> **The 4 Steps at a Glance**
>
> Step 1 — Understand the current situation (What is really going on?)
>
> Step 2 — Identify the root cause of the problem (What is actually causing this?)
>
> Step 3 — Develop an effective action plan (What specifically should we do?)
>
> Step 4 — Execute, monitor, and adjust (Do it — then keep improving)


### Step 1: Understand the Current Situation

Most people skip or rush this step, jumping straight to solutions based on surface-level symptoms. Watanabe insists on deep situational understanding before any analysis begins.

**What to do:** Gather data. Break the problem into its component parts. Compare categories and subcategories to see where the real gap exists — not just the aggregate picture.

**Classic mistake:** A student whose math grades drop assumes 'I need to quit soccer and study more.' But this assumption is based on the symptom, not the situation. Maybe only geometry scores are dropping, and only in specific sub-topics.

**Agent instruction:** Before proposing any solution, always ask: 'What is the full current picture? What data do I have? What categories can I break this into? Where exactly is the gap?'

### Step 2: Identify the Root Cause

This is the most critical — and most commonly skipped — step. Treating symptoms without finding root causes leads to wasted effort and recurring problems.

**Medical analogy:** A doctor does not prescribe medicine based on a patient saying 'I feel bad.' They ask questions, take temperature, run tests, and diagnose before prescribing. Problem-solvers must do the same.

**The process:**

1.  List ALL potential root causes — use a logic tree to ensure completeness.

2.  Form a hypothesis about the most likely root cause(s).

3.  Determine what information and analysis are needed to test the hypothesis.

4.  Collect data and analyze it to confirm or refute the hypothesis.

5.  Identify the confirmed root cause(s).

**Key principle:** Never stop at the first explanation. Keep asking 'why' until you reach an actionable, specific root cause. 'My math grades are dropping' → 'My geometry scores specifically' → 'My cylinder volume problems' → 'I never understood the formula.'

**Agent instruction:** When presented with a problem, always generate a complete list of possible causes before selecting the most likely one. Ask: 'What else could be causing this?' Use a logic tree to ensure no causes are missed.

### Step 3: Develop an Effective Action Plan

Once the root cause is confirmed, the solution almost writes itself — but the plan must be specific, not vague. 'Study more' is not an action plan. 'Practice cylinder volume problems for 30 minutes on Monday and Wednesday mornings' is.

**What a good action plan includes:**

- Specific actions (not general intentions)

- Who is responsible for each action

- When each action will happen

- What success looks like (measurable outcome)

- What resources are needed

**Generate multiple options first:** Before committing to a plan, generate a wide variety of possible solutions using logic trees, pros/cons lists, or criteria tables. Select the best combination.

**Prioritize actions:** Not all actions have equal impact. Prioritize those with the highest impact relative to cost and effort. Use a 2x2 impact/effort matrix if helpful.

**Agent instruction:** Always translate a confirmed root cause into a specific, actionable plan. Avoid generic recommendations. Specify what, when, how, and by whom.

### Step 4: Execute, Monitor, and Adjust

Execution is not the end. It is the beginning of a feedback loop. Problem-solving kids execute their plans and constantly monitor results, adjusting course when the evidence demands it.

**Key behaviors during execution:**

- Track progress against the specific success criteria defined in Step 3.

- Look for early signals — are the numbers moving in the right direction?

- Be willing to modify the plan when it is not working — but do this based on data, not impatience.

- If the plan fails, restart the process from Step 1 with new information.

**Feedback as growth:** Every outcome — success or failure — is data. Problem-solving kids treat failures not as setbacks, but as learning opportunities. This is what drives their compound growth rate.

**Agent instruction:** After recommending or implementing an action plan, define what to measure and when. Build in a review checkpoint. If results don't match expectations, go back to Step 2 before escalating.

# 4. The Problem-Solving Toolkit

Watanabe provides six core tools that support the four-step process. These are the same tools used by McKinsey consultants to analyze complex business problems — simplified for universal use.

### Tool 1: The Logic Tree

A logic tree is a visual branching diagram that helps break a problem, cause, or solution space into all its component parts — ensuring nothing is missed and no categories overlap.

**Primary uses:**

- Mapping all possible root causes of a problem (diagnostic tree)

- Generating a comprehensive set of potential solutions (solution tree)

- Breaking down any complex concept into manageable sub-components

**Two rules for a valid logic tree:**

6.  MECE — Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive: branches must not overlap, and together they must cover all possibilities.

7.  Move from general to specific: start with broad categories on the left, branch into more specific sub-categories to the right.

**How to build one:**

8.  Write the problem or question on the far left.

9.  Brainstorm: list every factor, cause, or solution you can think of on paper.

10. Group similar items together and label each group as a branch.

11. For each branch, ask 'What specifically?' to add sub-branches.

12. For each sub-branch, ask 'What can I call this group?' to verify grouping.

13. Check for MECE: are all possibilities covered? Does any item fall in two branches?

> **Logic Tree Example: Why Are Concert Attendance Numbers Low?**
>
> LOW ATTENDANCE
>
> ├── People are NOT AWARE of the concert
>
> │ ├── No advertising
>
> │ └── Advertising not reaching target audience
>
> └── People ARE AWARE but NOT ATTENDING
>
> ├── Timing conflict (concert on weekdays)
>
> └── Not interested in the show (repetitive set, no variety)


### Tool 2: The Yes/No Tree

A yes/no tree is a specific type of logic tree that segments people, objects, or situations into groups based on answers to binary yes/no questions. It is especially useful for identifying which group has the problem and for developing targeted solutions for each group.

**Example (Concert Case Study):**

- Q: Are you aware of the Mushroom Lovers' concerts? → Yes / No

- If No → Problem: awareness. Solution: advertising.

- If Yes → Q: Have you ever attended? → Yes / No

- If No → Problem: motivation. Solution: improve show appeal or schedule.

- If Yes → Q: Do you still attend? → Yes / No

- If No → Problem: retention. Solution: add variety, keep show fresh.

By segmenting the audience this way, the band can see that they actually have three different sub-problems requiring three different solutions — not one generic 'get more people' problem.

### Tool 3: Problem-Solving Design Plan

The problem-solving design plan is a structured table used to organize hypotheses, rationales, and planned analyses in one place. It ensures that information collection is purposeful — gathered to test specific hypotheses, not gathered randomly.

| **Issue / Question**     | **Hypothesis (What I think is causing it)** | **Rationale (Why I think this)**            | **Analysis / Actions to Test Hypothesis**             | **Information Source**            |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Why are grades dropping? | Geometry scores are the main drag           | Algebra and fractions scores are flat       | Break down math scores by sub-topic                   | Test score data by category       |
| Why is geometry poor?    | Weak on cylinder volume specifically        | Got 4/10 on cylinder problems last test     | Practice cylinder volume problems; get tutor feedback | Past tests, teacher feedback      |
| How to fix it?           | Extra practice + better study approach      | Same time studying, different method needed | Try 30-min morning sessions with new textbook         | Student, teacher, textbook review |

Using this table prevents 'analysis paralysis' — you only collect information you actually need to test your hypotheses, and every data point has a clear purpose.

### Tool 4: The Hypothesis Pyramid

The hypothesis pyramid is used to structure an argument or recommendation before presenting it to others — or before diving into data collection. It ensures that your logic is airtight and your communication is efficient.

**Structure:** The conclusion or main message sits at the top of the pyramid. All supporting rationales are arrayed below, like the base of a pyramid supporting its apex.

> **Hypothesis Pyramid Example**
>
> TOP (Conclusion): The Mushroom Lovers should move concerts to Saturday evenings
>
> and add 2-3 new songs per show.
>
> RATIONALE 1: 60% of students surveyed say weekday timing is a barrier to attendance.
>
> RATIONALE 2: All 3 repeat non-attendees said repetitive setlists caused them to stop coming.
>
> RATIONALE 3: Saturday timing conflicts with no major school activities.


Before collecting any data, draft the pyramid with your current best hypothesis. This forces clarity of thinking and shows you exactly what information you need to confirm or refute.

### Tool 5: Pros and Cons List

When multiple options exist and you need to choose among them, a structured pros and cons list provides a systematic comparison. Unlike a casual list, Watanabe's version incorporates weighting.

**4-step process:**

14. List all options under consideration.

15. For each option, list all pros and cons.

16. Assign a weight to each pro and con (e.g., +++, ++, + for pros; ---, --, - for cons) based on importance.

17. Select the option with the most weighted advantage overall.

This tool is especially useful for decisions with clear trade-offs, such as 'Which school should Kiwi attend?' where quality, cost, distance, and program strength must all be weighed.

### Tool 6: Criteria and Evaluation Table

When comparing multiple options against multiple criteria — especially when criteria have different levels of importance — the criteria and evaluation table provides the most rigorous comparison.

**5-step process:**

18. List all options.

19. Define evaluation criteria (e.g., quality, cost, location, reputation).

20. Assign an importance weight to each criterion (e.g., High / Medium / Low or 3/2/1).

21. Score each option against each weighted criterion.

22. Tally scores and select the highest-rated option.

| **Criterion**         | **Weight** | **School A** | **School B** | **School C** | **School D** |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Coaching Quality      | High (3)   | ★★★          | ★★☆          | ★★★          | ★☆☆          |
| Program Cost          | Medium (2) | ★☆☆          | ★★★          | ★★☆          | ★★★          |
| Location / Proximity  | Low (1)    | ★★☆          | ★★☆          | ★★★          | ★☆☆          |
| Team/Player Community | Medium (2) | ★★★          | ★★☆          | ★★☆          | ★☆☆          |
| **WEIGHTED TOTAL**    | **—**      | **22**       | **17**       | **21**       | **11**       |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|

This approach removes guesswork from multi-factor decisions and makes the reasoning transparent and auditable — critical for any agent that needs to justify its recommendations.

# 5. The Three Case Studies

Watanabe illustrates every tool and step through three extended case studies. Each study demonstrates the complete four-step process in a different life context.

### Case Study 1: The Mushroom Lovers — Root Cause Analysis

**Scenario:** A three-person high school rock band (Miss Mushroom, Eggplant, and Tofu) wants to fill their school gymnasium at monthly concerts. Attendance is low.

### Step 1 — Understand the Situation

Eggplant and Tofu gather basic data: How many people attend? Has attendance gone up or down? Who are the attendees? They discover the gymnasium holds 200 people but they average only 40-50 per show.

### Step 2 — Identify Root Cause

They build a yes/no tree to segment the school population:

- Group A — Not aware of the concerts (~40% of students)

- Group B — Aware but have never attended (~35% of students)

- Group C — Attended at least once, but stopped coming (~15%)

- Group D — Regular attendees (~10%)

They then conduct targeted interviews with each group (not a full school survey — just 5-10 people per group is enough for reliable patterns). Key findings:

- Group A: Never saw any advertising — problem is awareness.

- Group B: Interested, but concerts are on weekday evenings when they have homework — problem is scheduling.

- Group C: All three said 'You keep playing the same songs in the same way. Got boring.' — problem is show quality/variety.

- Group D: Very satisfied — not the focus for improvement.

### Step 3 — Action Plan

They create a logic tree of all possible ways to address each root cause, then prioritize:

- Awareness: Flyers in classrooms, announcement at school assembly, social media posts, email to school list.

- Scheduling: Move concert to Saturday evening — no homework conflict.

- Variety: Add 3 new songs per show; change set order; add one cover song.

### Step 4 — Execute and Monitor

They implement all actions and track attendance over the next two months. Attendance rises from 48 to 110 to 175. They identify that awareness drove the biggest initial jump; scheduling change sustained growth; and variety improvements converted one-time visitors into regulars.

### Case Study 2: John Octopus — Goal Setting and Budgeting

**Scenario:** John wants to become a CGI animator and Hollywood movie director. He needs to buy a computer as his first step. He has limited savings and needs to decide which computer to buy, what he can afford, and how to make up any funding gap.

**The Importance of Specific Goals**

Watanabe uses this case to illustrate that vague goals produce vague plans. 'I want a computer' is not a goal. 'I need a Mac Pro with specific specifications to run Cinema 4D by September 1, costing $2,400, and I have $800 saved' is a goal — it immediately shows the gap ($1,600) and sets up the action planning question: 'How do I raise $1,600 by September?'

**Building the Budget Tree**

John uses a logic tree to map all his possible income sources and cost-reduction strategies:

- Increase income: part-time job, odd jobs (lawn mowing, dog walking), selling unused items, asking family for birthday money as a lump sum.

- Reduce required amount: find a refurbished model, identify which specs can be lower without hurting performance, wait for a sale.

**Prioritization Matrix**

John evaluates each option using a pros and cons list weighted by feasibility and impact. He discovers that a combination of a part-time job (4 hours/week) + selling old gaming equipment + asking for early birthday money from grandparents will cover the gap by his target date.

**Key Lesson**

A clear, specific goal exposes the gap and makes the action plan obvious. The clearer the destination, the clearer the path. Problem-solving starts with ruthless specificity about what you want.

### Case Study 3: Kiwi — Choosing a Soccer School in Brazil

**Scenario:** Kiwi is an aspiring soccer player who wants to attend the best soccer school in Brazil to develop her skills. Multiple schools are available. She must select the best one for her specific goals.

**Defining Evaluation Criteria**

Before looking at schools, Kiwi defines what matters most to her. Using the criteria and evaluation framework, she identifies five criteria:

23. Coaching quality — HIGH importance

24. Competitive level of other players — HIGH importance

25. Cost of the program — MEDIUM importance

26. Distance from home — MEDIUM importance

27. Living facilities / safety — LOW importance

**Information Collection**

She builds a problem-solving design plan to determine what information she needs for each criterion and where to get it. She visits two schools, calls two others, and reads published rankings for the fifth. She collects only the information needed to test her evaluation matrix.

**Decision and Action Plan**

Using the weighted criteria table, School A scores highest. But it also reveals a funding gap: School A costs 30% more than her family's budget. Rather than accepting a lower-ranked school, Kiwi maps out a solution — she applies for one partial scholarship, asks her coach to write a letter of recommendation, and identifies a local grant program. She gets the scholarship and attends School A.

**Key Lesson**

The criteria and evaluation tool separates emotion from analysis. It makes the 'best' option visible and objective — and often reveals a path to get what you actually want rather than defaulting to a compromise.

# 6. How to Apply the Framework — Step-by-Step Agent Guide

This section translates the Problem Solving 101 framework into a practical operating guide for an AI agent or human practitioner. Use this as a decision protocol for any problem.

## 6.1 Diagnostic Protocol — Before Solving Anything

Before touching a tool, answer these questions:

| **Question to Ask**                                         | **What It Uncovers**                                 |
|---|---|
| **What exactly is the problem?**                            | Forces specificity; avoids solving the wrong problem |
|---|---|
| **What data do I have?**                                    | Identifies information gaps before guessing          |
|---|---|
| **What categories can this be broken into?**                | Prepares for logic tree construction                 |
|---|---|
| **What do I think is causing this?**                        | Surfaces initial hypothesis for testing              |
|---|---|
| **What would I need to prove or disprove that hypothesis?** | Drives targeted information collection               |
|---|---|
| **What would a good outcome look like specifically?**       | Defines success criteria for Step 4 monitoring       |
|---|---|

## 6.2 Tool Selection Guide

Match the tool to the task:

| **When You Need To...**                                | **Use This Tool**                                         |
|---|---|
| **I need to find all possible causes or solutions**    | Logic Tree — map everything, ensure MECE                  |
|---|---|
| **I need to segment people or objects into groups**    | Yes/No Tree — binary branching by question                |
|---|---|
| **I need to organize my investigation before I begin** | Problem-Solving Design Plan — hypothesis + analysis table |
|---|---|
| **I need to build or communicate a recommendation**    | Hypothesis Pyramid — conclusion at top, rationale below   |
|---|---|
| **I have 2-4 options and clear pros/cons**             | Pros and Cons List (weighted)                             |
|---|---|
| **I have multiple options with multiple criteria**     | Criteria and Evaluation Table                             |
|---|---|
| **I need to check if my plan is working**              | Review Step 4 metrics; restart Step 1 if needed           |
|---|---|

## 6.3 The Problem-Solving Loop

Problem solving is not linear. It is a loop. After Step 4, you always return to Step 1 to assess whether the problem is solved and what new information has emerged. This loop is what drives continuous improvement.

> **The Problem-Solving Loop**
>
> Step 1: Understand situation → Step 2: Find root cause
>
> ↑ ↓
>
> Step 4: Execute & monitor ← Step 3: Build action plan
>
> If the problem persists after Step 4: return to Step 1 with new data.
>
> If the plan is working: continue monitoring and look for next improvement.


## 6.4 Common Pitfalls to Avoid

**Pitfall 1 — Solving the symptom, not the root cause.** Prescribing before diagnosing. Always complete Step 2 before Step 3.

**Pitfall 2 — Collecting information without hypotheses.** Gathering data randomly wastes time. Use the design plan: only collect what tests a hypothesis.

**Pitfall 3 — Vague action plans.** "Work harder" or "do better" are not action plans. Every action must be specific, timely, and measurable.

**Pitfall 4 — Mr. Go-Getter syndrome.** Acting on intuition without analysis. Speed is valuable, but direction matters more than pace.

**Pitfall 5 — Not monitoring after execution.** A plan without measurement is wishful thinking. Define what to track before you start.

**Pitfall 6 — Building incomplete logic trees.** Violating MECE leads to missed root causes. Always check: are these branches exhaustive? Do they overlap?

# 7. Key Principles, Insights & Notable Quotes

## 7.1 Core Principles

The following principles distill the philosophical and practical core of Ken Watanabe's framework:

**Problem solving is a habit, not a talent.** Anyone can learn it. It requires the right attitude, the right tools, and consistent practice — not exceptional intelligence.

**Thinking and acting must be combined.** Thinking without acting = Miss Dreamer. Acting without thinking = Mr. Go-Getter. Problem-solving requires both, in the right sequence.

**Specificity unlocks solutions.** Vague problems produce vague solutions. The more precisely you can state the problem, the more obvious the solution becomes.

**Root causes are rarely what they appear.** Surface symptoms are not root causes. Effective problem-solvers dig past the first answer — they keep asking 'why' until they reach something actionable and specific.

**Data collection must be hypothesis-driven.** Collect information to test a specific hypothesis, not to explore randomly. This saves time and improves the quality of the conclusion.

**Feedback is the engine of growth.** Every result — positive or negative — is a learning opportunity. Problem-solvers who reflect after every action grow exponentially faster than those who don't.

**Small consistent improvements compound.** A 10% improvement rate produces 30x more output than a 1% rate over 3 years. Developing problem-solving habits has compounding returns over a lifetime.

**The best plans are adjustable.** No plan survives contact with reality unchanged. Build in review cycles and be willing to modify — but do so based on evidence, not emotion.

1. Can I state the confirmed root cause in one specific, precise sentence?

# 9. Supplementary Context — Author Background & External Insights

## 9.1 From McKinsey to Education Reform

Ken Watanabe's trajectory is itself a case study in the book's core thesis. At McKinsey, he used structured frameworks — logic trees, hypothesis pyramids, issue trees — as daily tools for diagnosing corporate problems. These were not academic exercises; they were battle-tested methods for navigating complexity under time pressure.

When he turned his attention to Japanese education in 2007, his insight was that these same tools — stripped of jargon and made visual and concrete — could be taught to anyone, at any age. The book was his proof of concept.

What surprised him was the response from adults. Japan's business community had never been explicitly taught these thinking skills, despite spending careers making consequential decisions. The book's adult readership vindicated his core premise: structured problem-solving is a universally under-taught, universally applicable skill.

## 9.2 Delta Studios — Applied Problem Solving in Education

After the book's success, Watanabe founded Delta Studios in Tokyo. The company's work is a living demonstration of the Problem Solving 101 philosophy: rather than teaching the framework in a classroom, Watanabe immerses children in real business operations (a mobile food van, product development, competitive selling) and then uses the framework retrospectively to explain what happened and how they could improve.

This approach — experience first, framework second — mirrors how Warren Buffett's childhood business experiences shaped his financial intuition before he ever studied finance formally. Watanabe's pedagogy deliberately prioritizes embodied learning over abstract instruction.

## 9.3 The McKinsey Connection — Where the Tools Come From

The tools in Problem Solving 101 are simplified versions of McKinsey's consulting methodology. The logic tree in the book corresponds to McKinsey's 'issue tree' — a standard tool for decomposing business problems. The problem-solving design plan mirrors McKinsey's 'work plan,' which maps hypotheses to analyses to owners to deadlines. The hypothesis pyramid is a version of McKinsey's 'pyramid principle,' made famous by Barbara Minto, which structures communication from conclusion to supporting argument, developed at McKinsey in the 1970s.

What Watanabe did was not invent new tools — he translated elite institutional knowledge into a form accessible to a 12-year-old. This translation is itself an act of brilliant problem solving: identify the real barrier (complexity of language, not complexity of concept), and design around it.

# 10. Agent Operating Instructions

This section specifies how an AI agent should use this knowledge base in practice. It translates the Problem Solving 101 framework into agent behavior protocols.

## 10.1 Default Problem-Solving Behavior

When a user presents a problem, challenge, or decision, the agent should default to the following behavior sequence:

1. Clarify the problem: Ask 'What is the specific outcome you want?' if the goal is vague.

1. Assess Step 1: What data or context does the user have? What is the current situation?

1. Apply logic tree thinking: Mentally map all possible root causes before responding.

1. Form a hypothesis: Identify the most likely root cause based on available information.

1. Recommend targeted analysis: If the hypothesis needs testing, suggest what information to gather.

1. Build a specific plan: When root cause is identified, produce a concrete, actionable plan.

1. Define success metrics: Specify how the user will know if the plan is working.

## 10.2 Anti-Pattern Detection

The agent should recognize and gently redirect the following anti-patterns in user behavior:

- If the user wants to skip root cause analysis: 'Before we decide what to do, let us make sure we understand what is actually causing this. Let me walk through a few possible causes...'

- If the user is vague about their goal: 'Let us start by getting specific about what success looks like. What exactly do you want to achieve, and by when?'

- If the user is stuck in analysis: 'We have a solid enough picture to act. Here is a specific first step we can take today while we gather more information...'

- If a proposed solution does not address root cause: 'That addresses the symptom, but the root cause seems to be X. Here is what would fix the actual problem...'

## 10.3 Tool Deployment Rules

- Always complete Step 2 before Step 3. Never jump to action planning without root cause identification.

- Use logic trees whenever the user is stuck generating options — visually map the branches.

- Use the design plan format when the problem requires research — structure the investigation.

- Use the hypothesis pyramid when presenting a recommendation — lead with the conclusion.

- Use criteria and evaluation when comparing options — make weights and scores explicit.

- Always close with Step 4 guidance — define what to measure and when to review.

## 10.4 Mindset Reminders

> **Watanabe's Core Reminders for Any Problem-Solver**
>
> 1\. Problem solving is a habit. Build it through consistent application, not occasional bursts.
>
> 2\. Specificity is power. The more precise the problem statement, the clearer the path.
>
> 3\. Root causes first, always. Never prescribe before diagnosing.
>
> 4\. Act, then monitor. Plans are hypotheses. Execution is the test.
>
> 5\. Learn from every result. Feedback is the fuel of growth.
>
> 6\. Small improvements compound. A 10% monthly improvement = 30x output in 3 years.
